SECTION 06

Annual Report Insights 2020–2025

Thematic focus, headline findings, and structural shifts from each edition

2020 — 12TH EDITION
COVID-19 & Pension System Resilience
The 2020 index covered 39 systems in its first year as the Mercer CFA Institute rebranded collaboration. Key focus: how funded vs pay-as-you-go systems weathered the pandemic shock. Netherlands led at 82.6. Average score dipped slightly due to sustainability concerns amid COVID fiscal pressures.
39 Systems · Netherlands #1
2021 — 13TH EDITION
Gender Pension Gap
Focus theme: gender differences in pension outcomes. The average score rose 1.0 point across all three sub-indices. Iceland climbed to joint first with Netherlands. Key finding: women face up to 40% lower retirement incomes in many systems due to career breaks and part-time work patterns.
43 Systems · Gender Focus
2022 — 14TH EDITION
Inflation Shock & Retirement Security
Rising inflation and interest rates created a new market dynamic challenging pension plans globally. Iceland took #1 for the first time. Indonesia upgraded from D to C. The report highlighted risks to DC savers from market volatility and the need for inflation-linked benefits.
44 Systems · Inflation Theme
2023 — 15TH EDITION
AI & Retirement Systems Innovation
Netherlands regained #1 (85.0). Focus on AI's potential to improve pension outcomes through better investment decisions and member engagement. Singapore jumped two places globally to 7th — achieving its first B+ grade. Indonesia upgraded again to C. Average score continued steady improvement.
47 Systems · AI & Innovation
2024 — 16TH EDITION
Demographic Pressure & DB→DC Shift
For the first time, people aged 65+ outnumber children under five globally. Netherlands retained #1 (84.8). Vietnam added as 48th system. Israel joined A-grade club. US scored C+ at rank 29/48. By 2080, over-65s will outnumber under-18s — structural urgency for reform.
48 Systems · Demographic Shift
2025 — 17TH EDITION
National Interest vs Fiduciary Duty
Singapore made history as the first Asian country to achieve A grade. Eight systems upgraded, zero downgraded. Four new entrants: Kuwait, Namibia, Oman, Panama. Focus: governments directing pension capital toward national priorities risks fiduciary integrity. Eight guiding principles proposed.
52 Systems · Singapore Historic A
Key Structural Trends Across All Six Years
PERSISTENT THEMES FROM CONSECUTIVE ANNUAL REPORTS
01
DB to DC Migration: The global shift from employer-guaranteed DB plans to individual DC plans is accelerating, concentrating longevity and investment risk with individuals.
02
Demographic Headwinds: Falling birth rates and rising life expectancy place mounting pressure on pay-as-you-go systems. By 2080, over-65s will outnumber under-18s globally.
03
Coverage Gaps: In voluntary systems like the US 401(k), large portions of the workforce — particularly informal and gig workers — remain without adequate pension coverage.
04
ESG & National Mandates: Governments are increasingly pressuring pension funds to invest in domestic infrastructure, ESG assets, or national priorities, creating tension with fiduciary duty.
05
Mandatory Contributions Win: Systems with mandatory contributions (Australia, Netherlands, Singapore) consistently outperform voluntary systems — a key reform lever for lagging nations.
06
Technology & AI: Digital tools and AI present an emerging opportunity to improve investment decision-making, member engagement, and cost efficiency across pension systems worldwide.
SECTION 07

Methodology & Framework

How the Mercer CFA Institute Global Pension Index is constructed

⚖️
Adequacy
40%
Measures the current benefits provided to retirees — including net replacement rates, minimum pension support, and system design features that improve long-term retirement outcomes.
🌱
Sustainability
35%
Assesses long-term viability — pension assets as % of GDP, contribution rates, coverage levels, demographic trends (old-age dependency), labour force participation, and government debt.
🔒
Integrity
25%
Evaluates governance, regulation, and protection — including regulatory quality, member communications, transparency of costs, governance standards, and protections for plan members.

Reform Recommendations — Universal Principles
EVIDENCE-BASED LEVERS FOR IMPROVING ANY PENSION SYSTEM
R1
Universal Coverage: Introduce or expand mandatory coverage to all workers, including self-employed and informal sector employees.
R2
Retirement Age: Gradually increase statutory retirement age in line with rising life expectancy to maintain actuarial balance.
R3
Minimum Pension Floor: Ensure adequate minimum support for the lowest-income retirees, reducing aged poverty rates.
R4
Asset Growth: Increase pension fund assets relative to GDP through higher mandatory contribution rates over time.
R5
Governance Standards: Strengthen regulatory frameworks, transparency requirements and member communications across all funds.
R6
Labour Participation: Encourage older workers to remain in the workforce longer through flexible retirement and phased transitions.
R7
Investment Freedom: Reduce restrictive investment mandates; systems with fewer restrictions consistently score higher on sustainability.
R8
Divorce Protections: Introduce pension-splitting provisions in divorce to protect — primarily women's — retirement outcomes.
